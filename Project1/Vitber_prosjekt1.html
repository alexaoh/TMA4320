Exercise 3.1:¶

We will show that equation (6) in the task description is a solution of the diffusion equation by simply inserting it into the equation with constant diffusion coefficient, since the coefficient $D$ is independent of x in the solution.

To show that

$\phi(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}} \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}}$

is a solution of

$\frac{\partial \phi(x, t)}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial^2\phi(x,t)}{\partial^2x}$,

we first calculate

$\frac{\partial \phi(x, t)}{\partial t} = -\frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}} \frac{1}{t} \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}} \frac{1}{t^2}\int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y) \, \frac{(x-y)^2}{4D}e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}}(\frac{1}{t^{2}} \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)\,\frac{(x-y)^2}{4D}e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}} - \frac{1}{2t} \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}})$.

Then we find

$\frac{\partial\phi(x,t)}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}} (-\frac{1}{4Dt}) \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)\,(2x-2y)e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}}$

$\implies \frac{\partial^2 \phi(x,t)}{\partial^2 x} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}}\frac{1}{4Dt}(-\int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)\,2e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}} + \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)\,\frac{(2x-2y)^2}{4Dt}e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}}) = \frac{1}{D\sqrt{4\pi Dt}}(\frac{1}{t^{2}} \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)\,\frac{(x-y)^2}{4D}e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}} - \frac{1}{2t} \int^{+\infty}_{-\infty}dy\,h(y)e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{4Dt}})$.

It is apparent that the expression (6) is a solution of the equation:

$\implies \frac{\partial \phi(x, t)}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial^2\phi(x,t)}{\partial^2x}$ $$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

By letting $t \longrightarrow 0$ in Eq. (6) it becomes apparent that the distribution $\phi (x,t)$ becomes narrower. It reduces to a very sharp line (quite like Dirac's Delta) around x = y. Therefore, one may conclude that $h(y)$ represents the initial distribution of the particles.

This solution (6) of the diffusion equation can also be found by applying a Fourier transform to the diffusion equation. By finding the solution this way, it becomes apparent from the beginning of the calculations that the function $h$ represents the initial distribution of the particles. Exercise 3.2:¶

The probability of finding a particle in position $x$ in a potential landscape $V(x)$ is given by $P(x) = Ze^{-\beta V(x)}$, where $Z$ is the canonical partition function of the system and $\beta = (k_B T)^{-1}$.

During a random walk the probability of a particle moving in the positive direction, staying put or moving in the negative direction is, respectively, $\{p^{+}, p^{0}, p^{-}\}$. Normalization dictates that their combined sum should equal one. This means the probabilities must satisfy the equation

\begin{equation}\label{eq1}\tag{1} p^{+} + p^{0} + p^{-} = 1. \end{equation}

We assume that each of the probabilities are proportional to $P(x)$ and that the proportionality constant, $C$, is equal for all steps. If the position before a random walk step is $x_0$ we get

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} p^{+} & =CP(x_0+1),\\ p^{0} & =CP(x_0),\\ p^{-} & =CP(x_0-1). \label{eq2} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

By putting these expressions into equation \eqref{eq1} we find the constant

\begin{equation}\label{eq3} C=\frac{1}{P(x_0+1)+P(x_0)+P(x_0-1)}, \end{equation}

and the desired probabilities

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} p^{+} &=\frac{P(x_0+1)}{P(x_0+1)+P(x_0)+P(x_0-1)} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\beta [V(x_0-1)-V(x_0+1)]}+e^{-\beta [V(x_0)-V(x_0+1)]}}, \\ p^{0} &=\frac{P(x_0)}{P(x_0+1)+P(x_0)+P(x_0-1)} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\beta [V(x_0-1)-V(x_0)]}+e^{-\beta [V(x_0+1)-V(x_0)]}}, \\ p^{-} &=\frac{P(x_0-1)}{P(x_0+1)+P(x_0)+P(x_0-1)} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\beta [V(x_0+1)-V(x_0-1)]}+e^{-\beta [V(x_0)-V(x_0-1)]}}. \label{eq4} \end{aligned} \end{equation}$$\tag*{$\blacksquare$}$$

Exercise 3.3:¶

With the condition

$k_B T \gg \left|V(x+1)-V(x) \right| = \left|\Delta V(x)\right| \implies \beta \left|\Delta V(x) \right| \ll 1,$

all the exponential terms will reduce to one in the first order series expansion of $\exp(x)$; $e^{-\beta \left|\Delta V(x) \right|} \approx e^{\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} \approx 1$. Since the condition must hold for any $x$, the difference in potential, $\left| \Delta V(x) \right|$, represents a difference for an arbitrary position $x$. By inserting $\left| \Delta V(x) \right|$ in the probabilities found in task 3.2 and taking the absolute value we get

\begin{equation}\label{eq5}\tag{2} p^{+} = \frac{1}{1+e^{2 \beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} + e^{\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|}} \approx \frac{1}{3}, \end{equation}\begin{equation}\label{eq6}\tag{3} p^{0} = \frac{1}{1+e^{\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} + e^{-\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|}} \approx \frac{1}{3}, \end{equation}\begin{equation}\label{eq7}\tag{4} p^{-} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-2 \beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} + e^{-\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|}} \approx \frac{1}{3}. \end{equation}

This seams rational since the potential is insignificant in relation to the thermal energy with the mentioned condition and therefore the particles must have equal probabilities to either stay put, move to left or to the right.

The other condition states $k_B T \ll \left|V(x+1)-V(x) \right| = \left|\Delta V(x)\right|$. From this we obatin $\beta \left|\Delta V(x) \right| \gg 1 \implies e^{\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} \approx \infty,$ and for the negative case $ -\beta \left|\Delta V(x) \right| \ll 1 \implies e^{-\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} \approx 0$.

With the same expressions for the probabilities as in equation \eqref{eq5}, \eqref{eq6} and \eqref{eq7} it becomes evident that $p^{+} \approx p^{0} \approx 0$ and

\begin{equation} p^{-} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-2 \beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|} + e^{-\beta \left| \Delta V(x) \right|}} \approx 1. \end{equation}

If the terms in the condition were arranged the opposite way, $k_B T \ll \left|V(x)-V(x+1) \right|$, the probability $p^{+}$ would instead go towards one and $p^{0} \approx p^{-} \approx 0$.

This shows that in the first case the potential is negligible, and in the second the thermal energy is neglible.

These approximations show that if the ratio between the potential and the thermal energy is high the thermal energy makes a negligible impact on the result compared to the potential energy. The energy scale of the potential energy is much larger than that of the thermal energy. Similarly, the potential energy makes a negligible impact on the results when the ratio is small. We shall deal with this ratio, $\beta k$, later.

            if p <= p_minus(new_array[position],V, betaAndK):

@jit(nopython=True)
def p_minus_cycle(x_0, V, alpha, beta_k, N_x): 
    '''
    Probability of random walk step to the left. 
    Uses periodic boundary conditions
    '''
    value = 0
    x_first = -N_x+h
    x_last = N_x
    if x_0 == x_first:
        value = 1/(1+e**(-beta_k*(V(x_0+1,alpha, N_x)-V(x_last, alpha, N_x)))+e**(-beta_k*(V(x_0,alpha,N_x)-V(x_last,alpha,N_x))))
    elif x_0 == x_last:
        value = 1/(1+e**(-beta_k*(V(x_first,alpha,N_x)-V(x_0-1,alpha,N_x)))+e**(-beta_k*(V(x_0,alpha,N_x)-V(x_0-1,alpha,N_x))))
    else:
        value = 1/(1+e**(-beta_k*(V(x_0+1,alpha,N_x)-V(x_0-1,alpha,N_x)))+e**(-beta_k*(V(x_0,alpha,N_x)-V(x_0-1,alpha,N_x))))
    return value

    x_last = N_x

Discussion 5.1¶

  • Why we get non-zero currents:

Since we do not have the symmetric case (where $\alpha = 0.5$) there has to be a non-zero current. As we have seen earlier, if the potential is strong enough the particles will always move towards lower potential. In this simulation $\beta k=1000$, which, based on the results in exercise 4, indeed can be said to be strong enough. In that exercise we clearly saw that the particles moved towards the lowest possible potential when $\beta k=100$, which implies that it is fair to expect that the potential dictates the movement of the particles in a greater manner at $\beta k=1000$. As we can see in the illustrations of the potentials, when $\alpha =0.8$ the sawtooth potential is leaning to the right, which means that the bottom is offset to the left. In other words, a particle de-exciting into the sawtooth potential will, on average, travel to the left, since it will move towards the bottom to minimize its potential. When $\alpha = 0.2$ we will have the opposite situation, where the potential leans leftward, resulting in a positive current. We can also see that the absolute values of the current are approximately equal whether $\alpha=0.8$ or $\alpha = 0.2$, due to symmetry.

  • Compare $J_{\mathrm{avg}}(0)$ with $J_{\mathrm{avg}}(n)$ for n > 0:

It is apparent that the averaged current during the first cycle is bigger in absolute value than the other cycle-averaged currents. The fact that the particles seek to minimize their potential energy can be used to give a physical interpretaion of this observation. The particles are the furthest from the positions of lowest potential in the beginning, when they are uniformly distributed. In contrast, all the consecutive cycles have an initial position where all the particles are in either one of the minimas. With this initial distribution the constant potential only distributes the particles isotropically with center at the minimas, but not uniformly along the entire x-axis. This is why the averaged current during the first cycle is larger, since, in the first cycle, more particles will relax to the minima, by a bigger amount of steps, than in all the consecutive cycles.

Programming exercise 5.2¶

The two x-coordinates which coincide with the minima of $V_1(x)$ must be $x = 0$ and $x = 0 + nN_x$, since it is periodic. The part of $V_1(x)$ which lies within $0 < x \leq \alpha N_x$ is a monotonically increasing function, which means that it's minima is when $x$ is the smallest in the given interval $\Rightarrow$ $x = 0$. The other part of $V_1(x)$ is a monotonically decreasing function, which means that it's minima is at the highest possible $x$ value in the interval $\Rightarrow$ x = 0. However, because of the periodicity of the function, the other minima is at $x = N_x$.

Comparison and comment on the graphs:¶

The analytical and numerical solutions seem to be strongly correlated, but seem to deviate from each other for larger values of T_p.

The shape of the graph in the two endpoints $T_p = 80$ and $T_p = 1500$ can be explained by the two following effects. Firstly, when $T_p$ increases, more particles will have "time" to be distributed around the x-axis in the constant potential. This leads to a higher averaged current since a larger amount of particles have to move more steps to relax to the potential minima. This phenomenon was described in task 5.2.

Secondly, as described earlier, $\beta k$ is so large that we can assume that for every time step in the sawtooth potential all the particles will move towards one of the minimas. With $N_x=10$, the maximum amount of time steps needed to move each particle to one of the minimas is $8$, because $\alpha =0.8$. Hereafter, each particle will stay still, and therefore not contribute to a larger $J_{\mathrm{avg}}$. Since we continue increasing $T_p$, the divisor in $J_{\mathrm{avg}}$ increases. This causes the absolute value of the average current to slowly decrease as $T_p$ increases.

Because the diffusion process distributes the particles very slowly around the center (the standard deviation increases slowly), the second effect will be dominant when $T_p$ is large, that is when $T_p > N_x$. The average current therefore decreases, and goes towards zero. This is in accordance with both the numerical and the analytical graphs.

The minima of $J_{\mathrm{avg}}$ could be found numericallly for each case of $N_x$, but we can clearly see that that the average current reaches it's maximum absolute value at some point after $T_p = N_x$. The reason why the graphs only increase in the plot is that $J_{\mathrm{avg}}$ reaches its minima before the interval $[80,1500]$, where the graphs are plotted. With a higher value for $N_x$ the minima exists at a higher $T_p$, for example at $T_p \approx 300$ when $N_x = 90$, as we can see in task 5.2.

Exercise 6.4¶

In section 6 in the task description, the following is stated:

"When the particles find themselves in the sawtooth-shaped potential $V_1(x)$, they will relax towards the potential minima. Hence, as the particles are excited to the second energy level defined by $V_2(x)$, they start out localized at positions $x_n = nN_x$, $n ∈ Z$."

In other words, it is assumed that the particles in the sawtooth-shaped potential $V_1$ will only be influenced by the strength of the potential, and not by the thermal energy. The thermal energy is completely neglected in the analytical argument. This assumption does not hold under the circumstance where $\beta k << 1$. However, it holds when $\beta k >> 1$, which is why the numerical values fit the theoretical values better for larger values of $\beta k$, as is apparent from 6.3.

As we saw in 6.2, the analytical $J_{\mathrm{avg}}$ diverges from the numerical value when $T_p$ is large compared to $N_x$. This can be attributed to the fact that the analytical argument assumes that the particles contributing to the current only move a maximum length of $N_x$, i.e. to either of the two minimas closest to the starting position. Although this is not explicitly stated in the "analytical argument", it is obvious from the mathematical expression for the average current

$J_{\mathrm{Avg}} = \frac{N_x}{2T_p}\sqrt{\frac{3}{4\pi T_p}}(\int^{+\infty}_{\alpha N_x}dx\,e^{-\frac{3x^2}{4T_p}} - \int^{-(1-\alpha)N_x}_{-\infty}dx\,e^{-\frac{3x^2}{4T_p}}) =\frac{N_x}{2T_p}(n^+ - n^-)$,

where the difference between $n^+$ and $n^-$ is multiplied by $N_x$. The assumption that the particles only move one minima to either side does, however, not hold when $T_p$ is large compared to $N_x$. This is because the particles have more time to diffuse in the constant potential which (in the numerical case) results in some particles diffusing past the neighbouring "valleys", thus contributing to the positive/negative current with $nN_x$, where $n > 1$ (neglecting thermal energy). This explains why the numerical $J_{\mathrm{avg}}$ is greater in absolute value than the analytical for large $T_p$ relative to $N_x$.

To summarize, the analytical solution includes implicit assumptions that $\beta k$ is large (the thermal energy is negligible) and that $T_p$ is not large compared to $N_x$.
